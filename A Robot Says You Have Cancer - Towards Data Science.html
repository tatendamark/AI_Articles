A Robot Says You Have Cancer

Sam Westreich, PhD
Apr 11 · 4 min read

If a computer gave you health advice, would you trust it? If a computer program told you that you have an aggressive cancerous tumor and recommended a risky procedure to save your life, would you take the chance?

Just a couple of decades ago, the idea of taking medical advice from a computer seemed like something out of an old Jetsons cartoon, or an episode of Star Trek. Medicine is complex, and it’s hard to imagine a computer program managing to successfully take the place of a human doctor, able to combine many different reports (test results, X-rays and other scans, and patient information) into a single diagnosis.

These days, however, the idea of a computer program acting as a doctor seems much closer to reality. But while computers may be growing better at diagnosing diseases, are humans ready to listen?

Computer Programs Can Match (or Exceed) Human Diagnosis Accuracy

An example of a complex medical problem being tackled by AI programs with surprising success is radiology, the practice of reading X-rays, CAT scans, and other visualizations of the inside of the body. These images are often blurry or indistinct, to the point that radiology has sometimes been described as “more of an art than a science”. Even experienced radiologists sometimes have difficulty determining whether a dark spot on an X-ray is a cancerous tumor, or just a smudge.

It turns out that computers can do a better job. If an algorithm is given a large set of X-rays to use as training data, it can outperform real doctors — in one case, an algorithm successfully diagnosed 75% of tuberculosis cases, compared to a rate of 62% correctly diagnosed by human doctors.

And it’s not just X-rays. AI algorithms successfully diagnosed skin cancer from photographs of moles and skin lesions at a rate equivalent to board-certified doctors. Another algorithm proved more effective at detecting arrhythmias (irregular heartbeats) by reading ECG data than human doctors. Some algorithms are even trying to tackle the diagnosis of cognitive impairment and mental decline by analyzing speech, using measures like pauses between words and the level of vocabulary to determine whether someone is suffering from dementia.

So computers can be more accurate than human doctors. Will anyone listen to them?

Do Doctors Need a Sense of Empathy?

Recently, a hospital in Fremont, California made the news — and not in a good way — when a 78-year-old patient was informed by a robot that he was dying.

Now, this wasn’t an AI-powered supercomputer. This was a human doctor communicating remotely with patients, using a telepresence robot. So despite the clickbait title, an artificial intelligence didn’t coldly inform a patient that he was dying — a human doctor chose to deliver this painful news over the equivalent of a FaceTime video call, instead of in person.

However, this article echoes a common theme that’s brought up any time there’s discussion of AI replacing doctors: algorithms don’t have a sense of empathy, and cannot deliver bad news or a tough diagnosis in the same way that a flesh-and-blood doctor can.

This article also raises the question of whether doctors are currently doing significantly better on the empathy front than robots. Many doctors are compassionate and caring when they deliver bad news to patients, but some already adopt a robotic, impersonal tone. Are these error-prone, low-empathy doctors going to be the first ones replaced by more accurate robots?

A Possible Future: Robot Diagnosis, Human Delivery

Looking forward, it’s likely that medicine will become more of a joint effort, with doctors leaning on computers for the heavy lifting of diagnosing diseases, but keeping a human present in the doctor-patient loop to provide explanations and comfort.

Of course, this concept isn’t likely to go down smoothly in the medical community. Shifts towards reliance on computers and algorithms for diagnosing medical diseases will likely raise questions regarding salaries and compensation — should a radiologist still be paid an average of $371,000 per year if the majority of their job is handled by a computer program?

Many doctors already struggle with technology intruding into medicine. There are widespread complaints about the increasing prevalence of electronic health records, or EHRs, which many doctors say end up being incomplete, difficult to fill out, and cannot replace face-to-face communication with a patient. Hospital administrators and executives, looking to both improve patient outcomes and save money, will likely champion increasing the use of algorithms to replace some of the workload handled by live doctors — but they’ll be fighting against doctors who, understandably, don’t want any part of their jobs taken by computers.

AI won’t completely replace doctors. Algorithms may prove better at diagnosing problems, but we may still want a human hand to write a prescription, to double-check a diagnosis, and most importantly, to communicate outcomes to patients with empathy and kindness.

But perhaps in the near future, when a doctor shows you an X-ray, you may be able to politely request a second opinion from a computer.
