    Why Some AI Research May Be Too Dangerous to Share

    Machine learning can be used to create weapons, ‘deepfakes’ and biased systems

    The Financial Times
    Jun 20 · 3 min read

    By Madhumita Murgia

    A recent feature in the FT Weekend Magazine about the UK spy agency GCHQ revealed to me one especially surprising titbit: the agency had been inspired to create an internet surveillance system by the Google-owned AI company DeepMind, based on an academic paper about an artificial chess grandmaster.

    According to a GCHQ official: “The people who did this at DeepMind, they published all the work, it’s out there, anybody can access it. So we should make use of it.”

    This touches on a thorny question that has been causing much debate among artificial-intelligence experts: should AI development be fully open-sourced, or are there ethical reasons to limit its accessibility?

    The argument centres on whether the code underlying machine-learning algorithms should be released in full into the public domain for the sake of academic transparency, or gated because of the potential for misuse.

    The two camps are polarised. The default practice so far in academia has been to release machine-learning models publicly so that others can reproduce and build on the results.

    Earlier this year, however, AI research outfit OpenAI created GPT-2, an algorithm that can write realistic and coherent text in a variety of styles (a news story, say, or a tweet or novel) based on a short prompt or headline.

    Though the algorithm was a benign academic effort, OpenAI decided it would be “too dangerous” to release its source code publicly. The reasoning was that criminals or other malicious actors could co-opt it to generate false news, spam or abuse. The decision caused an uproar in the academic community; researchers took to Twitter to express their disdain.

    Meanwhile, DeepMind said it had not actually publicly released the source code of AlphaZero, its chess-playing AI; it too has received criticism for not open-sourcing everything it creates.

    The company told me its reasons for not publishing the algorithm were not to do with security concerns, but acknowledged that the AI academic community is slowly starting to consider what “responsible publishing” looks like in an era when machine learning can be used to create weapons, deepfakes (images or videos that are hyper-realistic but totally false) and biased decision-making systems.

    “The machine-learning community really, really needs to start talking openly about our standards for ethical research release. We need a venue that is not Twitter,” wrote Ryan Lowe, a PhD student at McGill University, in the wake of the OpenAI controversy.

    Specialists have pointed out several reasons to wall off algorithms. Researchers including Michael Veale at the Alan Turing Institute and Robert Munro of AI translation company Lilt have written about how models can be reverse-engineered to extract the data that was used to train them. Veale describes the phenomenon as “algorithms that remember”, suggesting that facial-recognition algorithms written without proper safeguards might recall, and recognise, the faces of people whose photos were used to train them.

    Nick Bostrom, an Oxford university philosopher who writes on the risks of superintelligent systems, argues that openness can accelerate AI development in the medium term, but making the area too competitive also gives the market less time and incentive to put safety controls in place. “Partial openness that enables outsiders to contribute to an AI project’s safety work and to supervise organisational plans and goals appears desirable,” he writes.

    Machine learning is already being applied to areas as diverse as loan applications and policing, where experts acknowledge it is imperfect. Applications such as facial recognition can be inaccurate and biased. Hiring or credit-scoring algorithms can exacerbate social inequities due to biased data. Deepfakes can cause political chaos.

    AI is only going to become more powerful and ubiquitous. Now is the moment for academics to step outside the research bubble and acknowledge that the software they write is changing people’s lives. The time for treating AI as a theoretical science is over.
